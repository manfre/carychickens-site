Council Moves Chicken Ordinance Forward

April '1220

Tonight, the council had many votes related to the potential chicken ordinance. The most important vote was that they agreed to move forward with legalizing chickens. There were also votes regarding the number of hens, fees, and setbacks.

Going in to the meeting, these were the ordinance recommendations from us, council, and town staff.

Cary Chickens Recommendation

  • 10 hens
  • 10’ setback
  • Low yearly fee ($10/year) or higher one time fee ($30)

Council’s Guidelines from February Meeting

  • 8 hens
  • 10’ setback
  • $10 yearly fee

Town Staff Recommendation From Report

  • 4 hens
  • Varies based upon zoning (e.g. 8’ sides, 22’ rear for one zoning and 10’ sides and 25’ rear for another)
  • $50 yearly fee

The ordinance has not been finalized, but the council votes ended up somewhere between the council guidelines and the staff recommendation with:

  • 6 hens
  • Varies based upon zoning (e.g. 8’ sides, 22’ rear for one zoning and 10’ sides and 25’ rear for another)
  • $50 initial fee with details about ongoing fees pending

Number of Hens

When discussing this issue with council members prior to the February meeting, we wanted 10 hens, but settled on 8. The reasoning behind our desire for 10 hens was to account for the decreasing egg yield from hens as they age. With 8-10 hens, a family could start with 4 and then add 2 new hens every other year. As the flock approaches the 8 or 10 hen limit, the first 4 hens would begin to die of old age and the average age of the flock would be ~3 years old. Average maximum age of a hen that is 100% safe from predators is 6-8 years.

Year 0: 4 hens (4 laying at peak)
Year 2: 6 hens (~6 laying at peak)
Year 4: 8 hens (~5 laying at peak)
Year 6: 6-8 hens (~5 laying at peak)
Year 8: 6-8 hens (~5 laying at peak)

Note: All towns in the triangle that have legalized hens allow at least 10

Setbacks

A flat setback is easy to write, interpret, enforce, and explain. Town staff will be able to consistently say “coop must be 10’ from the property line” without needing to look up the zoning of a specific address, which takes staff time.

Fees

Town staff based their fee suggestion on temporary sign/usage fees, instead of the more closely related dog and cat fees. They also stated during the council meeting that they did not do any research to determine how much it would cost to legalize chickens (registration, staff time to handle complains, etc).

It costs as little as $5 to register a dog or cat with the town (spayed/neutered with microchip), and that registration is a one-time fee valid for the life of the animal. With the dog and cat registration, you must include veterinary contact info and proof of vaccination.

The registration process/form for chickens has yet to be determined, but needs to be defined before a reasonable fee can be set. A $50 yearly fee to collect a name, address, and check is not reasonable.

Alissa has done a significant amount of research in to the number of calls received by various towns that have legalized chickens. Based upon the town’s population and current animal control call volume, we anticipate less than 10 legitimate issue calls per year. An issue is along the lines of “chicken roaming free”, “neighbor has a rooster”, etc. The initial year will have a higher number of “my neighbor has chickens!” complaints, but those will subside as town residents learn of the new ordinance change.

Final Thoughts

It’s great that an ordinance to legalize hens in the town is moving forward, but we feel the ordinance could use some tweaks for the benefit of town staff and future chicken owners. Now is the time to call and email town council to let them know that you want an ordinance that is easy to understand, easy for the town to enforce, and that will finally put this issue to rest after almost 4 years in the spotlight.

Mark your calendars, May 24th will be the public hearing during the council meeting.
